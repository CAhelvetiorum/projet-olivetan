Historical Context

Line 1-3
This opening passage reflects the rhetorical conventions of 16th-century moral treatises, particularly influenced by classical stoicism.
Line 8
The term "vertue" here follows Middle English spelling conventions common in manuscripts from this period.
Line 12-15
Biblical allusion to Ecclesiastes 3:1, "To every thing there is a season," demonstrates the author's theological background.

Main Text (Base Manuscript)

1Of all the vertues that adorne the soule of man, none
2is more excellent than prudence, which guideth us
3in all our actions toward that ende most happie.
4
5For what availeth it to have knowledge of worlde
6matters, if we want the skill to use them well?
7What profiteth learning without discretion?
8Or what good can come of wit wanting vertue?
9
10Therefore let us embrace this noble gift, which
11maketh us like unto the angels, and separateth
12us from the beasts of the field. For there is
13a time for all things under heaven, and blessed
14is he that knoweth when to speak and when
15to hold his peace.
